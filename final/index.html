Predicting the Quality of White Wine
Introduction:

One of the most ancient alcoholic beverages, white wine has been a mainstay in Western cultures with its unique flavors being a defining factor in its popularity. As a result of its popularity, the white wine industry has taken great worth, with a value of over $40 billion in the United States market alone. When considering the quality of a specific sample of white wine, multiple elements take part, such as acidity, sugar content, and pH.

Problem Definition:

With its history, comes a group of wine tasters claiming that they can tell the best wines apart from the others. While they do have the experience, we hope to create a machine learning model that can help beginner wine tasters choose better wines over others – without the years of experience required.

Data Collection:

The White Wine Quality dataset from Kaggle was sourced for the project, providing data on over 4000 samples of white wine, including an evaluation of the sample quality and the following eleven features: fixed acidity, volatile acidity, citric acid, residual sugar, chlorides, free sulfur dioxide, total sulfur dioxide, density, pH, sulfates, and alcohol.

When first examining the dataset, no explicit cleaning was required as it was absent of formatting errors and missing data points. The data then underwent preprocessing via standardization and feature reduction via principal-component analysis. However, through the course of the project, it became apparent that output quality of the dataset suffered from clustering. The quality of the individual wine samples was evaluated on a scale from 0 to 10. However, as seen in Figure 1, no samples provided had qualities of 0, 1, 2, or 10, and an overwhelming majority of samples were of qualities 5, 6, and 7.

Figure 1: Graph of qualities of sample against sulfates and citric acids

Purple points indicate quality of 3, blue points indicate quality of 4, orange points indicate quality of 5, red points indicate quality of 6, yellow points indicate quality of 7, green points indicate quality of 8, black points indicate quality of 9

In order to account for the data skewing, we decided to redefine how the quality of wine would be measured. Instead of defining quality as a scale from 0 to 10, we decided to label wine as either “poor quality” with a value of 0 or “good quality” with a value of 1. Values between 3 and 6 were reassigned to 0 while values between 7 and 9 were reassigned to 1. In doing so, the over-representation of wine qualities 5, 6, and 7 could be counteracted which would thus improve the performances of the prediction models overall.

Methods:

For the quantification and analysis of white wine quality, we decided to implement three unique models; the models would then be compared against one another in terms of accuracy and performance to determine the best for categorizing white wine based on the chosen features. The three models that were decided were a regression model, a random forest model, and a neural network. The regression model was chosen due to its ability to assist in finding a relationship between the characteristic variables and, thus, their relationship to wine quality. The random forest model was chosen due to its pattern of high-accuracy results. The neural network was chosen due to its ability to establish thorough connections between the dataset and and the determined results.

When deciding upon the regression model, it became apparent that there were multiple options to consider and thereby decided to compare the results of two individual models in order to choose the best regression model for the data. The two chosen for comparison were linear regression and logistic regression. Logistic regression was chosen due to it performing well in classification situations, which our data falls under since quality is defined as either 0 for poor quality or 1 for good quality. Since logistic regression can be considered as an extension of the functionality of linear regression, we decided linear regression would be a good choice to compare logistic against.

From the figures, we can see that the linear regression model only managed an average F1-score of 0.80 while the logistic regression model was able to reach an average F1-score of 0.84. While both scores are of an acceptable magnitude, the logistic regression model proves itself to yield higher accuracy results in comparison to the linear regression model. However, when comparing the performances, the linear regression model vastly outperforms the logistic regression model. When creating fifty models, the linear regression model had an average training time of 0.373 seconds each, whereas the logistic regression model had an average training time of 5.359 seconds each. Thus, while the logistic model edged over the linear regression model in terms of accuracy, its performance was less than desirable.

After the implementation of the regression models, we then began an random forest model, hoping to reach similar or greater accuracies to the logistic regression model without the heavy usage cost. The random forest model achieved even greater accuracy than the logistic regression model, performing with an average F1-score of 0.89, indicating strong ability to predict the quality of wine from the given features. In addition, the random forest model achieved a performance of 0.456 seconds each of training time on average when tested in the creation of fifty models. This greatly outperforms the logistic regression model and is similar to the linear regression model, but with the added benefit of much greater accuracy.

While the neural network achieved an approvable average F1-score of 0.82, it failed to surpass both the logistic regression model and the random forest model. In addition, the neural network achieved a performance of 2.501 seconds each of training time when tested in the creation of fifty models which, while outperforming the logistic regression model, failed to outdo the random forest model. With the random forest model as the forerunner in terms of balance between performance and accuracy, we decided to use the model to evaluate an alternative split for when a wine classified as poor or good; specifically, we decided to test whether there would be significant change if the maximum value for poor wine was shifted down to 5 instead of 6 in the quality feature of the original dataset. Below in Figure 6 are the produced results from utilizing the alternative splits with a random forest model analyzing the wine qualities.

Figure 6: Resultant information and accuracies from random forest model; Maximum poor wine quality is 5

With the alternative maximum wine quality for poor wine, the accuracy of the random forest model decreased, with the average F1-score decreasing from 0.89 to 0.81. We determined there was excessive overlap for it to be reliably separated and the scale was not reliably consistent and thus, we decided to keep the original splits with the maximum wine quality to classify for poor wine being a 6.

From the confusion matrices for each model, there is a consistent trend of a high quantity of True Negatives ranging in the 600s or 700s while the True Positives are smaller, ranging only in the 200s. In addition, for each confusion matrix there was a higher quantity of False Positives than there were False Negatives. The confusion matrices also indicate that the random forest model has the least error of the four models we chose to use with 229 errors.

Conclusions:

With the final random forest model, classification of the white wines based on the predetermined features into either poor quality or good quality can be done with an accuracy of 89%, which is of an acceptable level for use. However, when considering a more nuanced prediction where the wine is evaluated on a scale, our current model implementation cannot accurately do so. For further development in order to create a model that would be capable of doing so, a primary priority would be to locate more data values to diversify the dataset and completely use the given range.

To further increment the capabilities of the model, more features can be considered and evaluated for their correlation to quality; those that greatly affect the quality can then be considered for the model performance to gain a more nuanced understanding of the factors of wine quality and, thus, the prediction of white wine quality without tasting.
