Predicting the Quality of White Wine
Introduction:

Wine is a popular drink that has been around for thousands of years. Many factors influence the quality of wine including acidity, residual sugar, chlorides, pH, etc. By observing these characteristics we can predict qualities of certain white wines. One example would be how grapes grown in warmer regions produce “bolder and fruitier” wines due to the grapes ripening easier in these areas. This results in lower acidity levels and higher sugar levels in the grape itself (Gambetta). The Dataset that we are using for this project is from Kaggle which calculates a quality score of Northern Portugal wine samples from 0 to 10 based on 11 properties.

Problem Definition:

With its history, comes a group of wine tasters claiming that they can tell the best wines apart from the others. While they do have the experience, we hope to create a machine learning model that can help beginner wine tasters choose better wines over others – without the years of experience required.

Methods:

To quantify the white wine quality, a supervised learning model will be implemented. We intend to utilize a regression model due to its ability to assist in finding a relationship between all of the characteristic variables and thus their relationship to the wine quality. A random forest model will also be implemented due to its history of high-accuracy results. The third model to be implemented will be a neural network; they can establish thorough connections between the dataset and the determined results which may exceed the accuracy of the non-linear regression model. 4898 samples will be considered in the dataset, ensuring enough variety for accurate training and testing.

Results and Discussion:

        To analyze the potential results regarding the wine quality predictions, each of the developed models’ accuracies will be determined using a subset of the dataset designated for testing; through this comparison the model best-suited for wine prediction can be determined. With an accurate model, one may evaluate the quality of wine without the prior experience that is usually necessary. With an effective model, the white wine industry can implement the research to grade their products, eliminating the potential bias from human testing and expediting the testing process. For future applications, additional parameters for the wine can be examined to detect if more conditions affect the wine quality. Furthermore, red wines and more alcohol types can be considered for future developments.

        Before the implementation of a prediction model, the dataset was examined to determine if data cleaning was necessary; the data, however, did not require explicit cleaning as it was absent of formatting errors and missing data points. Preprocessing was then applied to the dataset via standardization and through feature reduction through a principal component analysis.

        When we began implementing a regression model, it became apparent that there were multiple options of regression to consider, such as linear regression, ridge regression, and logistic regression. Both ridge regression and logistic regression can be thought of as extensions of the functionality of linear regression. Thus, linear regression was decided upon to be chosen as a potential regression model to compare against. In our dataset, the score of the wine quality is defined by values from 0 to 10. Since the values lie directly on the integers, it can be considered to be discretely defined rather than continuously defined. As a result, logistic regression was chosen as a potential regression model due to it usually performing well in classification situations.

        Both models were implemented, with the accuracies compared against when fine-tuning the model parameters: polynomial degree and considered features. Below in Figure 1 is a scatterplot displaying the accuracy of the regression models against the polynomial degree used for the polynomial fitting for the regression. When examining the results of the scatterplot, it is apparent that the logistic regression model is consistently more accurate at classifying the testing data in comparison to the linear regression model. The figure also demonstrates that, once we achieve polynomial degrees of 5 or greater, the accuracy in classifying the testing data suffers for both models. Thus, the most optimal polynomial degree is determined to be 4.

Below in Figure 2 is a scatterplot displaying the accuracy of the regression models against the dimension size, or the number of features considered when utilizing the model. When examining the trends in the scatterplot, we see that the logistic training and logistic testing accuracies are consistently greater than the linear training and linear testing accuracies respectively. Therefore, we will proceed with logistic regression in consideration. While the high logistic training accuracy when using 11 dimensions may initially appear to suggest overfitting of our model, we see that the logistic testing accuracy is maximized when using all 11 dimensions. Thus, we determine that the optimal number of dimensions from our dataset to consider in our logistic model is 11.

In Figure 3, we printed the training and test accuracy from the 11 dimension - logistic regression we created, giving us a .97 training accuracy and a .63 testing accuracy. The high training accuracy refers to the accuracy of the logistic model on the subset of data it was constructed on while the testing accuracy refers to the accuracy of the logistic model on data it hadn’t already used. We determine that our logistic regression model does have some application for our final results but, for now, realize there is room for  improvement to find a model with an even better fit.
